Impressionism

The term "impressionism" has come to refer to the work created between the 1860s and the end of the 19th century that questioned the norms of conventional fine art, challenges that were raised in various ways by various artists.

About Impressionism

Impressionism is an art movement that originated during the 19th century, mostly in France. It is known for capturing the effects of light and atmosphere. The movement marked a significant departure from tradional academic painting styles at the time. Paintings during this movement had thin, small, yet visible brush strokes.

Woman With A Parasol

Monet uses light, impulsive brushstrokes to add color splashes. The wind is blowing Mrs. Monet's veil and her billowing white dress; the green bottom of her parasol echoes the meadow's waving grass. With a powerful upward viewpoint and appearing as though from below, she is viewed against fluffy white clouds against an azure sky. In order to provide depth, a boy—Jan, Monet's seven-year-old son—is positioned farther away, hidden behind a hill in the landscape and only visible from the waist up. Monet used bold, lively brushstrokes to capture the fleeting nature of the moment.

Glossary

Impressionism got its when the title that Claude Monet gave to one of his paintings is where the word "impressionism" first appeared. It was a whimsical choice that happened in an instant, fitting the name of a kind of art that concentrates on capturing transient moments.
